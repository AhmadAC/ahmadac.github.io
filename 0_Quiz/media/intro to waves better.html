Intro to Waves

Properties, Behaviors, and the Mathematics of Waves

1. The Two Main Types of Waves

Mechanical Waves (e.g., Sound)

Waves that REQUIRE a medium (particles like solid, liquid, or gas) to travel. They transfer energy by bumping particles into each other.

  • Cannot travel through a vacuum (empty space).
  • Travel fastest in solids (particles are close together).
  • Travel slowest in gases (particles are spread out).

Electromagnetic (EM) Waves (e.g., Light)

Waves made of vibrating electric and magnetic fields. They DO NOT require a medium to travel.

  • Can travel perfectly through a complete vacuum (like outer space).
  • Travel at the speed of light (300,000,000 m/s in space).
  • Includes Visible Light, Radio Waves, X-Rays, and Microwaves.

2. The Electromagnetic Spectrum

The Electromagnetic Spectrum charts EM waves based on their frequency and wavelength (λ). Notice how red represents lower energy (longer wavelength λ), while blue and purple represent higher energy (shorter wavelength λ).

The Doppler Effect (Sound)

When a source moves towards you, the waves bunch up (decreasing λ, increasing frequency). When it moves away, they spread out (increasing λ, decreasing frequency).

5. Light in the Cosmos: Redshift & Emission Spectra

The Doppler Effect applies to light too! When a star or galaxy moves towards us, its light waves bunch up, shifting toward the blue end of the spectrum (Blueshift). When it moves away from us, the waves stretch out, shifting toward the red end (Redshift).

How do we know? Elements like Hydrogen act like fingerprints, absorbing and emitting light only at very specific wavelengths (Emission Spectra). By looking at where these "fingerprint" lines appear through a telescope, we can calculate exactly how fast the star is moving!

6. Quantum Energy Levels & Emission

Why do elements only emit specific wavelengths of light? In the quantum world, electrons orbit the nucleus only at specific, allowed energy levels (n). They cannot exist in between.

When an excited electron falls from a higher orbit to a lower one, it loses energy. This exact packet of energy is released as a Photon (a particle of light). The color (wavelength λ) of the photon depends precisely on how big the energy drop was.

Photon Energy Equation:

E = (h × c) / λ
E = Energy (Joules)
h = Planck's Constant (6.626 × 10-34 J·s)
c = Speed of Light (3 × 108 m/s)
λ = Wavelength (meters)
Example: Hydrogen Red Line (n=3 to n=2)
Drop Energy: ΔE ≈ 3.03 × 10-19 Joules
λ = (h × c) / E
λ = (6.626×10-34 × 3×108) / 3.03×10-19
λ ≈ 656 × 10-9 m (656 nm = Red!)

7. Thermal Radiation & Wien's Law

Absolutely everything with a temperature above absolute zero emits electromagnetic waves (Thermal Radiation). As an object gets hotter, two things happen: it emits more total energy, and the peak wavelength (λ) shifts toward the blue end (shorter wavelength).

This is why a piece of heated metal goes from glowing dull red, to orange, to bright white-blue as it gets hotter! We use Wien's Displacement Law to calculate the temperature of distant stars just by looking at their peak color.

Wien's Displacement Law:

λpeak = (2.898 × 10-3) / T
λpeak = Peak Wavelength (meters)
T = Temperature in Kelvin (K)
b = Wien's Constant (2.898 × 10-3 m·K)
Example: Calculating the Sun's Surface Temp
We observe the Sun's peak emission at λ = 500 nm.
T = (2.898 × 10-3) / (500 × 10-9)
T ≈ 5,796 K

8. Wave Optics: Young's Double Slit Experiment

In 1801, Thomas Young proved that light is a wave. If you shine a single color of light (like a laser) through two tiny, closely spaced slits, the light waves diffract and overlap on the other side.

Where the crests of both waves meet, they build each other up (Constructive Interference = Bright Fringes). Where a crest meets a trough, they cancel out (Destructive Interference = Dark Fringes).

Fringe Position Equation:

y = (λ × L) / d
y = Distance from center to 1st bright fringe (m)
λ = Wavelength of light (m)
L = Distance to the screen (m)
d = Distance between the two slits (m)
Example Calculation:
Red Laser (λ = 600 nm = 6×10-7 m)
Screen is L = 2.0 m away.
Slits are d = 0.5 mm (5×10-4 m) apart.
y = (6×10-7 × 2.0) / (5×10-4)
y = 0.0024 m (2.4 mm spacing)

9. The Math Lab: Universal Wave Equation

The Universal Wave Equation

v = f × λ
v = Velocity / Speed (m/s)
f = Frequency (Hz)
λ = Wavelength (meters)

Rule of thumb: In a specific medium, speed (v) stays the same. So if Frequency goes UP, Wavelength (λ) must go DOWN to balance the equation!
